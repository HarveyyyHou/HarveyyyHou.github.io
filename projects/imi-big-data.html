Scotiabank × UofT Big Data & AI Competition

An end-to-end anti-money laundering analytics solution.

Project summary: This project was completed as Team 88 for the IMI Big Data & AI Competition. Our team built an end-to-end anti-money laundering solution combining an AML knowledge library, data cleaning, feature engineering, clustering, unsupervised anomaly detection, rule-based risk scoring, score fusion, customer-level explanations, and a GUI explanation viewer. Top 5 Finalist from 430+ PhD, Master's, and undergraduate students across 90+ teams from the University of Toronto, University of Toronto Mississauga and University of Toronto Scarborough. Note: We have the least amount of team members out of the 5 finalist teams. Therefore more work is distributed to all team members.

Team members: Danny Han, Edward Junsyoung Lee, and Junyi Hou.

Code for this project is available upon email request.

Project Goal

The goal of the project was to identify potentially suspicious customers for AML review while keeping the system interpretable. Instead of relying only on black-box machine learning, the solution combined source backed AML typologies, engineered risk features, unsupervised anomaly detection, and rule based scoring.

Deliverables

1 AML Knowledge Library with red flags, typologies, source traceability, and feature mapping.
2 Bad-actor detection pipeline producing risk scores and flagged customer outputs.
3 Customer-level explanations describing why customers were flagged or not flagged.

AML Knowledge Library

The AML Knowledge Library served as the research foundation for the project. It organized money laundering and terrorist financing red flags into a structured reference system. The library focused on organized crime typologies and connected each red flag to source-backed documentation and possible data signals.

  • Built a structured AML knowledge base with 147+ red flags and indicators.
  • Covered typologies such as trade-based money laundering, bulk cash smuggling, oil smuggling, Chinese money laundering networks, synthetic opioids, underground banking, human trafficking, and general money laundering / terrorist financing indicators.
  • Mapped red flags to engineered features used later in the detection pipeline.
  • Supported interpretability by aligning model features and explanation categories with AML concepts.

Data Cleaning and Feature Engineering

The pipeline cleaned KYC and transaction data before building a master feature table. Feature engineering was guided by the AML Knowledge Library so that model inputs were tied to meaningful risk signals rather than arbitrary variables.

Feature Group Examples
Velocity Transaction counts, per-day rates, flow-through timing, sudden inflow/outflow patterns.
Amount / Structuring Round amounts, just-below-threshold counts, large transaction counts, structuring flags.
Channel Wire transfers, ABM/cash activity, EFT/EMT/card/cheque volumes, multi-channel behavior.
Geographic Country/province/city diversity, cross-border flags, high-risk jurisdiction indicators.
Behavioral / Profile Lifestyle mismatch, missing KYC, account age, business age, shell/cash-intensive/MSB indicators.

Clustering and Customer Segmentation

Kmeans Clustering was used to segment customers by behavior and support risk interpretation. The project used dimensionality reduction and clustering to identify groups of customers with similar transaction and profile patterns.

  • Used Factor Analysis for Mixed Data (FAMD) to reduce mixed numerical and categorical features.
  • Applied K-means clustering to create customer segments.
  • Used silhouette score and elbow inertia to support model selection.
  • Explored separate segmentation for individual and business customers.
  • Used selective inference concepts to evaluate whether cluster differences were statistically meaningful. Meaningful clusters may receive a small boost in score.

Machine Learning Models

Because labeled suspicious/non-suspicious outcomes were limited, the project emphasized unsupervised anomaly detection methods. These models were used together with rule-based scoring rather than replacing AML logic.

  • Local Outlier Factor (LOF)
  • Angle-Based Outlier Detection (ABOD)
  • Isolation Forest

The ML models helped identify unusual customers within the engineered feature space, while the rule-based system preserved interpretability and connection to AML red flags.

Rule-Based Risk Scoring

The rule-based algorithm produced interpretable risk scores using five AML-aligned categories. Each category used feature thresholds and weights connected to the AML Knowledge Library.

Risk Category Weight Purpose
Structuring & Amount 25% Captures suspicious amounts, round values, and just-below-threshold activity.
Channel 25% Captures risky transaction channels such as cash, wire, Western Union, and multi-channel behavior.
Geographic 20% Captures cross-border behavior and high-risk jurisdiction exposure.
Behavioral 20% Captures flow-through activity, lifestyle mismatch, sudden inflow/outflow, and unusual behavior.
Profile 10% Captures business/profile risk indicators such as MSB, shell company, and cash-intensive flags.

Fusion Algorithm

The fusion algorithm combined rule-based risk scores with unsupervised anomaly scores. This design allowed the project to balance explainability and anomaly detection.

The final risk score used a weighted combination of rule-based and machine learning scores. Customers in the top 5% by risk score were flagged for review.

Explanation Generator and GUI Viewer

The explanation generator created short, non-technical explanations for each customer. The goal was to make the outputs usable by AML staff or reviewers, not only by data scientists.

  • Generated one explanation per customer.
  • Explained whether the customer was flagged or not flagged.
  • Listed contributing risk categories such as structuring, channel, geographic, behavioral, and profile risk.
  • Avoided unnecessary machine learning jargon.
  • Supported a GUI viewer for browsing and searching customer explanations.
