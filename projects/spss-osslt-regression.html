SPSS & R OSSLT Regression Analysis

A multiple linear regression analysis in SPSS and R using Ontario school level OSSLT data.

Project summary: This project analyzed Ontario school-level OSSLT Test first attempt pass rates using SPSS. The analysis studied how school type, school language, special education services, low-income household percentage, and parental education level relate to OSSLT outcomes. The workflow includes data cleaning, variable recoding, descriptive statistics, multiple linear regression, diagnostic checks, response transformation, model comparison, influence diagnostics, outlier removal, and final model interpretation. Note: The final report PDF is built from Rmd(Rstudio).

Research Question

How do school type, school language, special education services, family income, and parental education level influence OSSLT first-attempt pass rates in Ontario schools?

Data

The analysis used Ontario school-level data. The original dataset contained 4,926 observations. After selecting relevant variables and removing incomplete cases, the cleaned analysis dataset contained 737 schools.

Variables

  • Response variable: OSSLT first-attempt pass rate.
  • Categorical predictors: school type and school language.
  • Numerical predictors: special education percentage, low-income household percentage, and percentage of students whose parents have no degree/diploma/certificate.

SPSS Workflow

  • Imported the raw dataset into IBM SPSS.
  • Selected and renamed variables used in the regression analysis.
  • Converted numeric-looking string variables into numeric SPSS variables.
  • Created dummy variables for categorical predictors.
  • Generated descriptive statistics, frequency tables, bar charts, and histograms.
  • Ran preliminary multiple linear regression.
  • Saved predicted values and residuals for diagnostics.
  • Created transformed response variables: log, square root, and squared OSSLT pass rate.
  • Compared models using R², adjusted R², RSS, AIC, and BIC.
  • Added a squared special education term to model a nonlinear relationship.
  • Checked standardized residuals, studentized residuals, leverage, Cook’s distance, and DFFITS.
  • Removed influential observations and fit the final model.

Preliminary Model

The preliminary multiple linear regression model used the untransformed OSSLT pass rate as the response variable. The model included school type, school language, special education percentage, low-income percentage, and parental education percentage.

The preliminary model explained approximately 54.5% of the variation in OSSLT pass rates. School language, special education percentage, low-income percentage, and parental education were statistically significant predictors, while school type was not statistically significant.

Predictor Coefficient p-value Interpretation
Public School -1.069 .101 Not statistically significant after accounting for other predictors.
French School 5.459 <.001 French-language schools were associated with higher OSSLT pass rates.
Special Education % -0.621 <.001 Higher special education percentage was associated with lower pass rates.
Low Income % -0.296 <.001 Higher low-income percentage was associated with lower pass rates.
No Parent Degree % -0.465 <.001 Higher percentage of parents without post-secondary credentials was associated with lower pass rates.

After choosing the square-root-transformed response, I compared models with and without school type and with and without a squared special education term. Adding the squared special education term improved model fit, while school type contributed little once the other variables were included.

Influence Diagnostics and Outlier Removal

I used SPSS to check standardized residuals, studentized residuals, leverage, Cook’s distance, and DFFITS. The influence diagnostics identified a small number of cases with extreme or influential behavior. After removing selected influential observations, the model fit improved.

Final Model

The final SPSS model used the square-root-transformed OSSLT pass rate as the response variable and included school language, special education percentage, squared special education percentage, low-income percentage, and parental education percentage.

R² = .598 The final model explained about 59.8% of the variation in the square-root-transformed OSSLT pass rate.
Adjusted R² = .595 The adjusted model fit remained strong after accounting for predictor count.
AIC = 831.53 AIC improved substantially after transformation and outlier removal.
BIC = 863.66 BIC also improved in the final model.

Conclusion

The SPSS analysis identified socioeconomic indicators, special education concentration, and school language as meaningful predictors of OSSLT first-attempt success rates. School type was not retained in the final model because it contributed little after accounting for the other predictors.

This project also demonstrates practical SPSS skills that are useful in applied research support: importing data, cleaning variables, recoding categories, running regression models, saving residuals, checking assumptions, computing model comparison metrics, and exporting statistical output.
