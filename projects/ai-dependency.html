AI Dependency Survey Study

A quantitative study of AI reliance, trust, verification behavior, and usage motivations among university students.

Project summary: This project examined predictors of AI dependency among university students using survey data. The study focused on whether dependency is better explained by demographic characteristics, usage motivations, psychological beliefs, or exposure intensity. The results suggest that AI dependency is mainly belief-mediated: students who believe AI improves their performance and who trust AI over instructors tend to report stronger dependency.

Research Question

What factors predict AI dependency among university students, and how are trust, perceived performance improvement, usage purpose, and verification behavior related to students’ reliance on generative AI tools?

Data and Participants

The study collected online survey responses from university students about AI usage habits, beliefs, verification behavior, and demographic characteristics. The final analysis used responses from approximately 71 completed university-student surveys.

Measures

  • AI dependency: a nine-item composite scale measuring behavioral, emotional, and functional reliance on AI tools.
  • Psychological beliefs: perceived performance improvement, reduced verification behavior, and trust in AI relative to instructors.
  • Exposure intensity: weekly screen time and AI adoption timing.
  • Usage context: primary AI usage purpose and social media platform usage.

Methods

The analysis combined reliability assessment, factor analysis, correlation analysis, MANOVA/ANOVA, hierarchical regression modeling, and descriptive qualitative analysis of open-ended responses.

Key Results

~65% Final model explained approximately 65% of the variance in AI dependency.
α ≈ .77 The AI dependency scale showed acceptable internal consistency.
p = .0033 AI usage purpose significantly predicted AI dependency in ANOVA.
Belief predictors Performance improvement and trust in AI over instructors were strong predictors.

Usage Purpose and Dependency

AI usage purpose was significantly associated with dependency levels. Students using AI primarily for problem solving or task completion reported higher dependency than students using AI mainly for consultation. The ANOVA result was F(2, 68) = 6.21, p = .0033, with a medium effect size.

Hierarchical Regression Findings

Demographic variables explained little variance in AI dependency. Adding usage motivation and psychological belief variables substantially improved the model. In the final model, perceived performance improvement, trust in AI over instructors, and task-completion purpose were especially important predictors.

Qualitative Findings

Open-ended responses suggested that students frequently used AI for academic tasks such as writing, essays, papers, classwork, and assignments. The word “code” also appeared, suggesting technical and programming-related use. Words such as “verify” and “check” indicated that some students were aware of the need to evaluate AI-generated outputs, while terms such as “employment,” “application,” and “internship” showed that AI use extended into career-related activities.

Conclusion

The study suggests that AI dependency among university students is driven less by demographics or simple exposure and more by psychological beliefs. Students who view AI as performance-enhancing and who trust AI more than instructors are more likely to report dependency. These findings highlight the importance of AI literacy, trust calibration, and verification habits in higher education.
